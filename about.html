Soul Without
Apology
From singing "Gasolina" at age two to 14 million streams on Spotify, Zeke Pujols is proving that genuine talent cuts through the noise.

There is a moment on Can I Bother You? where Zeke Pujols switches effortlessly between English and Spanish, laying his Bronx origins and Honduran-Dominican heritage completely bare. It is not a calibrated crossover attempt. It is, in the most precise sense of the word, a portrait: a 23-year-old artist bridging two worlds, completely in his element.

This is who Zeke Pujols is. Born Ezequiel Pujols on March 23, 2002, the stage was always his resting pulse. Before he was a viral sensation with nearly a million TikTok followers, he was a 5th grader auditioning for "La Voz Kids," a kid picking up the guitar, and a high schooler gracing the Westport Country Playhouse in a production of "In the Heights."

"I didn't expect 'Every Night' to blow up the way it did," he tells me over the phone, referencing the massive single that helped define his breakout 2024-2025 run. "But I've been doing this since I was singing Daddy Yankee at family parties at age two."

"I've been doing this since I was singing Daddy Yankee at family parties at age two."

It was during the COVID-19 pandemic that his vocal covers and dance content on TikTok struck gold, amassing 25 million likes. But unlike many digital creators, his transition to original music wasn't a pivot—it was the destination. Tracks like "Who Can Blame Her" quickly amassed over 14 million streams on Spotify, confirming his audience was there for the artistry, not just the algorithmic novelty.

Can I Bother You?, his 8-track 2025 debut project, is the record that articulates that lifelong intersection. It is a confident Spanglish R&B project demonstrating that an artist born on social media can craft lasting, deeply musical work.

The production weaves contemporary R&B smoothness, pop hooks, and Latin percussion, with his voice sitting securely at the center. It's a significant leap for the young Bronx native, transitioning from a phone screen to global DSPs.

But Zeke isn't gambling. He is certain. Not with arrogance — with the quiet conviction of someone who has already put in his 10,000 hours behind the piano before the world ever hit follow.

Interview
P.38
Q & A
In His
Own Words
Q
You had your major breakthrough during the pandemic on TikTok. What was it like transitioning from a viral social media presence to a fully signed artist with NWS Music Group?
A
It's a bizarre jump. On TikTok, the feedback loop is instant. You post a cover or a dance, and thousands of people react immediately. But shifting to a single like Unconditional takes patience. NWS Music Group has been incredible in letting me take my time to find my actual sound—not just what works in a 15-second clip, but what works as a complete song. I want to prove I'm a real musician first.
Q
You sing in both English and Spanish, sometimes switching natively mid-track. How important is your Dominican heritage to your musical identity?
A
It's everything. I grew up in the Bronx, so those two worlds—the American R&B/Pop side and my Honduran/Dominican roots—are completely blended for me. There is no separation. When I write, the emotion dictates the language. Sometimes a feeling just hurts more in Spanish, you know? It's just honesty.
Q
Tracks like "Who Can Blame Her" crossed 14 million streams on Spotify. Did you know you had a hit when you were writing it with your guitar?
A
Not at all. You never really know. I was just sitting with my guitar—I play piano too, but the guitar is where I go to think. I played those chords and the melody just fell out. The fact that over 14 million people connected with a moment of me just being alone in my room... it's still hard to process.
Q
What do you want people to walk away with when they listen to the new project?
A
I want them to see the growth. I'm 23 now. I'm not just the kid doing covers anymore. I want people to hear the instrumentation, the bilingual flows, the actual R&B grit, and realize that I'm here to stay. This isn't a trend for me. It's just who I am.
